# Founding Era — Narration Device

Rutare Ventari is not a neutral narrator.

He writes as a man trying to place himself on the right side of history while slowly discovering that the act of writing exposes him. The narration device is therefore not just a frame. It is the character arc.

## Function

Rutare's confession allows the trilogy to reveal history through pressure instead of explanation.

At first, he describes everyone else with certainty. He judges his brothers, his ancestors, and the political machinery that followed them. He understands the damage clearly when it belongs to others.

But when the chain of blame moves closer to him, the prose begins to resist itself. His sentences become more defensive. His confidence fractures. What begins as record-keeping becomes self-exposure.

## Progression Across the Trilogy

### Film 1 — Selective Judgment

Rutare frames the early wound as something created by others. He watches Teresta, Ilunis, Viriatus, and Ecalus as if their flaws are visible from a safe distance.

He is sharp, moral, and controlled — but not yet honest.

### Film 2 — Forced Proximity

The story begins to implicate the family system more directly. Rutare can no longer describe events as isolated mistakes. Love, pride, shame, conquest, and inheritance start to reveal themselves as one connected wound.

The narration begins to flinch.

### Film 3 — Collapse of Innocence

By the end, Rutare can no longer write around the truth. The official story of Viriatus's death breaks open. Regulatus is revealed as a civilization built on a transformed lie.

Rutare's final movement is not victory. It is recognition.

## Design Principle

The device works because the narrator's failure is visible before he admits it.

The audience should feel the distance between what Rutare says, what he avoids, and what the causal chain proves.

His confession is not a window into history.

It is history fighting its way through a man who wants to survive his own memory.
