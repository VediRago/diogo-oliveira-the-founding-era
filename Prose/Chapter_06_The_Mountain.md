# Chapter Six: The Mountain

The last time I saw Teresta, we were by the well.

We were talking about universities. She didn't say where she was going. She knew me better than I knew myself.

"I'll miss you."

She glanced back at the house and left. I knew she wasn't expecting me to return the words, but even if there had been doubt, her stride erased it.

Father appeared.

They crossed paths — I remember that.

Nothing passed between them.

I wrote earlier that Teresta carried the same certainty as our father.

I was wrong.

I want to believe they knew, somewhere underneath it all. What they claimed reflected them.

Not her.

Our own for leverage.

She left.

I want to believe they knew, somewhere underneath it all.

"You leave tomorrow."

He pressed sealed notes into my hand.

If I questioned him?

I don't know.

No one told her she'd be missed.

Teresta was already gone from sight. I felt the urge to say something, but like now, like always, it came after.

I mouthed the same words she'd given me, half-hoping the wind would carry them, half-knowing I was just talking to myself.

A half century later, regret still comes too late.

The estate sat on land nobody wanted. Rocks and hills all the way to the mountain's base. New roads had been built. Almost three years. Just roads.

At the rise, a small fort stood, built from the same rock the hills were made of. Something out of nothing. I couldn't account for it.

Two Servans. Three trainers. Viriatus.

How do you raise a fort with that?

A trainer came toward me across the yard.

"To shape what needed shaping."

He said it like it meant something. Like the line had been earned.

"That's the line you must deliver to your father."

He didn't wait. Turned and walked.

"Keep up. Write everything."

We moved to Viriatus immediately.

"A phenomenon," the trainer said. "He can trigger a state. Used to come only under threat. Now it comes when pushed."

He opened a ledger and handed it to me without looking.

"Two days straight. The exercise was fifteen trees. He stopped at two hundred. He's still learning to speak, but from what I gathered, he forgot how to count past two hundred."

Two hundred trees. No rocks left on the ground. And a fort.

I counted the rocks in the outer wall.

Four hundred and fifty-one. Just the one wall.

But if I knew nothing — just looking at him — I'd have believed it was a life everyone longed for.

Then he saw me.

He dropped the drill mid-movement and ran over.

"Brother!" he called out.

He meant it. No performance in it. None of the calibration you'd expect from someone his age.

He walked me through the grounds, the horses, the food stores — proud, proprietary, like the showing itself was proof of something.

The trainer had to drag him back.

Viriatus laughed and returned without argument.

I documented everything. Wrote it exactly as instructed.

He never read the walls for what they were. Never saw the mechanism turning around him. To him, it was all prospect. All warmth.

I knew what happened to the people Father selected. I had watched the shape of it before.

Viriatus hadn't.

The gap between his understanding and mine widened with every day I spent there.

Father came without notice one afternoon.

Viriatus saw him and shed everything — dropped the training where it was and crossed the yard at a run, face open, nothing held back.

Father received him. Kindly, even.

I was close enough to see the measurement underneath.

Viriatus wasn't looking for it.

The estate settled back into its rhythm. Viriatus to work. Me to the reports. Then home.

The road gave me too much time.

I kept returning to Teresta not looking back. To Viriatus saying brother like the word had weight.

The official record had measurements, data, full documentation of the adrenaline state and what it cost the body. Complete. Precise.

The laugh when the trainer hauled him back didn't make it in. The word brother didn't either. Nor the look on Viriatus's face in the moment after Father turned away.

Those I carried home instead. The way I carried most things — without a name for them, and without putting them down.

Keeping everything on track left nothing over. Not even the question of what I needed to keep.

Writing this now, things come back I didn't know I'd kept.

A fool, then.

A fool now.
