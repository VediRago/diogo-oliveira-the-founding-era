# Chapter Three: The Sentence I Gave Him

Something about Ermerus repelled Father. Ecalus would not dismiss him entirely. That was not his way. He gave the same measure of attention to all who bore the Ventari name. But his eyes showed less. Ermerus received less. From less he came to desire more. Father was present in body. Absent in every other way. Even with me. And I was the useful one. The one who cost him nothing.

Ermerus learned to catch the exact instant it happened. Father's face would drain. Go flat. My brother was right about this. I watched it myself. I did not see what Father saw because I did not remember her. But Ermerus had Mother's smile. He was four the first time he changed it. And it worked.

That is the part I keep returning to. It worked. Father could look at him longer after that. And here is where I should have understood what was coming. A child who learns to disappear that early does not vanish. He waits.

That was the first lesson people taught Ermerus Ventari. He learned that something in him changed Father, yet he had not been told he had Mother's mouth. Then came the knowledge, from a sibling too young to know the damage a sentence could do. That sibling was me. Gods help me. I was the one who told him.

I was six, perhaps. Young enough to speak horror as if it were harmless. We were in the nursery. I remember light on the floor. I remember something small in his hands. I do not remember why I said it. That is one of the cruelties of memory. It stays and loses the reason. Mother died when you were born, I told him.

Ermerus looked at me. Only that. He looked at me, and whatever he held stopped moving between his fingers. I kept speaking. Of course I did. That is why Father gets strange near your birthday. He did not cry. He did not ask me what I meant. He did not run from the room. He only looked down again. And went back to playing. But not as before.

I have lived that moment countless times. I have tried to make myself kinder inside it. Silent inside it. But I was there. I said it. And after I said it, Ermerus understood something. That he had arrived with a death attached to him. That his face, his mouth, could warm the persistent winter that walked through those halls.

He watched Father's face in the moments before the stillness came. He watched me become careful. He watched Ilunis depart for the battlefields. He did not get to watch much of Teresta. She was the smart one. She went away. Each of us found a way to survive it. Ermerus learned to use the freedom of being unseen. I learned to be useful. Ilunis found purpose in war. Teresta learned that distance was safer.

And none of us knew yet what would happen with his arrival. Until that strange day.
